How Ready Do You Think
Ocampo Is For Natural Disasters?

Think Ocampo is ready for natural disasters? Put your perspective to the test!
Compare your assessment with real facts to uncover how prepared our municipality truly is.

The term ANDAM in Bikol means "ready" or "prepare," reflecting the Bicolano people's resilience and readiness to face challenges, particularly natural disasters. The word has Austronesian roots, resonating with similar terms across Philippine languages, symbolizing preparedness as a cultural value. In the disaster-prone Bicol region, where typhoons are frequent, being ANDAM is a way of life, shaping everything from local traditions to community planning efforts to foster safety and unity.

“Andam Ocampo” is a platform designed to help the community evaluate its disaster preparedness with an engaging and humorous twist. It's all about raising awareness with a sprinkle of fun!

Because your life depends on it! Knowing your community's disaster readiness can make the difference between "I’m prepared" and "Oh no, I forgot the flashlight!"

This website allows you to rate Ocampo’s disaster preparedness in various categories, and we compare your ratings with factual data, helping you understand the reality versus your perception.

Evacuation Plans

What's The Fact?

You rated . Based on our research, Ocampo has made substantial progress in disaster planning, particularly with the approval of its Municipal Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Plan (MDRRMP), which is used in briefings for calamity response and other proactive measures. The municipality has also developed a Climate and Disaster Risk Assessment (CDRA) for 2023, along with contingency plans for Tropical Cyclone Wind Signals. Additionally, DOST-PHIVOLCS has provided important hazard maps for Isarog Volcano, including lava flow, pyroclastic density current, and lahar hazard maps. The MDRRMO also distributed DRRM materials to all 25 barangays, including five tarpaulins per barangay featuring detailed flood hazard maps, landslide susceptibility maps, and Isarog Volcano-related hazard maps. In 10 remote barangays, these hazard maps are posted in visible locations, such as barangay halls and BDRRMC offices, to increase awareness among residents. Signage also guides people to evacuation centers in flood-prone and landslide-susceptible areas. Despite these efforts, there are still significant challenges in terms of public engagement and awareness, particularly at the barangay level. While hazard maps and signage are in place, their utilization and public awareness remain low in many areas. The MDRRMO’s online hazard map postings are not always clear, and there is a need for higher-quality, accessible copies of these maps. Furthermore, while signage exists, the practical use and understanding of these materials could be improved, especially in remote areas where access to resources may be limited. These steps show progress but also highlight the need for increased transparency, clearer communication, and greater public involvement. Improving the accessibility of disaster plans and hazard maps, as well as fostering better public engagement in disaster preparedness at the barangay level, would strengthen Ocampo’s overall disaster risk reduction efforts. We rate this as , acknowledging progress while emphasizing the need for improvement in public awareness and engagement.

Infrastructure Resilience

What's The Fact?

You rated 7. Based on our research, most public buildings in Ocampo, including evacuation centers, barangay halls, municipal halls, and public markets, are structurally strong and capable of withstanding typhoons with up to TC Wind Signal no. 3-4. These buildings, often made of concrete and well-engineered, can endure strong winds and heavy rainfall. However, some public buildings, such as elementary & secondary schools' classrooms and barangay chapels, which have commercially available grade B aluminum roofs, may suffer major damage or even complete destruction in stronger typhoons. Although ongoing construction and renovation efforts are improving the resilience of these buildings, there are still vulnerable structures that need attention. We rate this as 7.

What's The Fact?

You rated 8. Based on our research, Ocampo’s drainage systems are around 75% effective in preventing floods. Over the past five years, significant efforts have been made to reinforce drainage canals, with many projects funded by the LGU and supported by KALAHI-CIDSS. These canals, with depths of 3 to 5 feet, effectively manage rapid water flow from mountains and rivers. However, challenges persist, particularly in poblacion barangays where plastic pollution leads to clogging, resulting in foot-level floods during intense rains. Additionally, in low-lying areas near spillways and rivers, there have been incident reports of floodwaters reaching chest level during the onslaught of Typhoon Kristine (2024), especially when riprap structures overflow. While cleanup efforts and anti-littering laws are in place, consistent enforcement, improved waste management, and further infrastructure development are needed. We rate this as 8.

What's The Fact?

You rated 8. Based on our research, Ocampo’s roads and bridges are generally resilient during typhoons. National highways and major bridges are well-maintained and remain passable even during heavy rains and flooding, thanks to annual assessments and regular maintenance. However, challenges remain in rural barangays, where smaller and makeshift bridges are more vulnerable. During Typhoon Kristine (2024), a reported hanging bridge in one barangay was completely destroyed, and some makeshift rural bridges became too risky to cross due to structural damage and water flow. Additionally, rural roads near rivers became impassable due to the strong current and flooding, further complicating access. Reconstruction projects are underway to address these issues, with significant efforts to reinforce rural roads and bridges. While the overall infrastructure has shown resilience, particularly for major roads, there is still a need for continued upgrades to ensure that smaller rural infrastructure can withstand extreme weather conditions. We rate this as 8, recognizing the progress made while emphasizing the importance of ongoing improvements in rural infrastructure resilience.

Overall Preparedness

What's The Fact?

You rated 9. Based on our research, Ocampo’s MDRRMO demonstrates a robust and innovative use of technology in disaster preparedness and response. Their dedicated Facebook account, operational for over eight years, along with the municipality and mayor’s pages, effectively disseminates crucial updates and DRRM-related knowledge. These platforms not only serve as tools for raising public awareness but also document MDRRMO operations, projects, and activities consistently since 2017. During calamities, the MDRRMO employs technology to monitor weather conditions, provide real-time updates, and communicate advisories to residents. They rely on official forecasts and bulletins from PAGASA and EDMERO to guide their actions. This year, they advanced their capabilities by introducing camera drones to document and monitor affected areas, enhancing situational awareness and response efficiency. For typhoon advisories, traditional methods are combined with technology; vehicles equipped with megaphones make rounds to issue warnings and evacuation instructions. Additionally, Incident Response Management Team (IRMT) personnel use specialized geotagging-enabled camera apps for accurate documentation of incidents and resources, which facilitates effective coordination and reporting. However, despite these commendable efforts, there remains substantial room for improvement. The integration of emerging technologies such as AI-powered disaster forecasting tools, enhanced communication systems for faster broadcasting of advisories, and other advanced monitoring technologies could further optimize their response strategies. Access to such innovations would significantly boost the municipality's technological preparedness and capacity for disaster management. We rate this as 9.

What's The Fact?

You rated 8. Based on our research, Ocampo residents exhibit a commendable level of cooperation during typhoon events. The majority of the population adheres to directives issued by local authorities, particularly when it comes to evacuation and disaster preparedness measures. This cooperation stems from the consistent efforts of the Incident Response Management Team (IRMT), barangay officials, and the community’s overall awareness of disaster risks. Pre-emptive or forced evacuations are conducted in cases where residents show reluctance to leave their homes. These operations, spearheaded by MDRRMO officers, barangay councilors, and tanods, are especially targeted at high-risk zones such as flood-prone areas and landslide-prone communities. The involvement of the Philippine National Police (PNP) and the Armed Forces of the Philippines (AFP) in IRMT activities reinforces the seriousness of these efforts, as their authority commands compliance. Cooperation is particularly evident among residents who proactively evacuate, recognizing the risks posed by their geographic locations. However, challenges remain with certain individuals or families who are hesitant to leave their homes due to personal reasons or attachment to their property. Barangay officials work tirelessly to address these concerns, ensuring the safety of every resident through direct communication and community-level engagement. Over time, the municipality’s education campaigns and the dissemination of disaster preparedness information have cultivated a culture of cooperation and collective responsibility. This spirit of unity and responsiveness continues to strengthen Ocampo’s resilience against typhoon-related disasters. However, further efforts to encourage 100% compliance, especially through enhanced community education and support mechanisms, would be beneficial. Overall, the cooperation among residents is robust and significantly contributes to the municipality’s disaster management effectiveness. We rate this as 8.

Based on our research and observations, the Municipality of Ocampo demonstrates a proactive approach to disaster risk reduction, as evidenced by its comprehensive and systematic pre-disaster measures. These efforts significantly reduce risks and enhance the safety of residents in the face of potential disasters. The Incident Response Management Team (IRMT) and the Emergency Operations Center (EOC) are activated 24/7 during disaster-prone periods, ensuring real-time monitoring, quick decision-making, and immediate response. Specific preemptive measures have been institutionalized, such as: (1) activation of Barangay DRRMCs to monitor high-risk areas, especially those prone to landslides or flooding, (2) conducting preemptive evacuations in low-lying or flood-prone areas, (3) utilizing early warning systems like bandilyo (mobile public announcements) to disseminate critical advisories, (4) implementing safety protocols in evacuation centers, and (5) monitoring settlements near riverbanks to prevent water-related activities and mitigate erosion risks. The municipality also ensures constant communication between barangay officials and the MDRRMO through situation reports and text updates. Early planning, such as the preparation of contingency measures, underscores the proactive stance of local leaders. Regular updates from DOST-PAGASA, combined with vigilant monitoring by the MDRRMO, further strengthen disaster preparedness efforts. While these measures are commendable, there is always room for improvement. Increasing community awareness and participation in risk reduction activities, as well as leveraging advanced forecasting and simulation technologies, could elevate the municipality’s proactiveness to an even higher level. What's The Fact?

You rated 8. Based on our research and observations, the Municipality of Ocampo has established a structured and generally effective approach to recovery after typhoons. Once it is safe to return, evacuees are transported home using municipal vehicles, while road clearing operations are conducted to restore accessibility. CASURECO IV, the local electricity cooperative, promptly carries out line clearing operations to ensure the swift restoration of power. Barangay officials perform house-to-house inspections to assess damages, followed by the distribution of relief goods such as food, hygiene kits, and other essential items, prioritizing those in greatest need. Farmers whose crops are insured often receive aid to mitigate agricultural losses, and free medical services are provided to address health concerns post-disaster. Youth organizations, fraternities, and volunteers actively participate in donation drives, providing hot meals and distributing relief items, while schools conduct psychological aid sessions to help students recover mentally. The municipality also continues situational monitoring 24–48 hours after typhoons to evaluate recovery progress. However, some households report being overlooked during relief efforts, highlighting the need for better resource allocation, expanded supplies, and improved coordination among officials to ensure equitable distribution. Despite these challenges, the municipality’s collaborative efforts demonstrate strong commitment to recovery, with room for enhancements in efficiency and inclusivity. We rate this as 8.

What's The Fact?

Based on all the gathered information, Ocampo demonstrates commendable efforts in disaster preparedness, showcasing a robust and multi-faceted approach. The municipality’s MDRRMO is well-organized, with an active and responsive team supported by the coordinated efforts of barangay councils and the Incident Response Management Team (IRMT). Pre-disaster measures such as regular risk assessments, early warning systems, and preemptive evacuations highlight a proactive stance, as does the consistent activation of the Emergency Operations Center (EOC) during threats. The municipality’s use of social media and traditional methods like megaphone advisories ensures timely dissemination of information, and technological advancements, such as the introduction of drones for monitoring, bolster their response capabilities. Ocampo ranks 542nd out of 3rd to 4th class municipalities nationwide in the 2023 Cities and Municipalities Competitive Index, developed by the National Competitiveness Council through the Regional Competitiveness Committees (RCCs) with assistance from the United States Agency for International Development. This ranking highlights Ocampo’s overall competitiveness, with a particular focus on its 458th place in resiliency, showcasing its continuous improvement in disaster readiness. Last December 4, Ocampo was recognized as a FULLY COMPLIANT LGU awardee for the consecutive year through the Office of Civil Defense Region 5 during the 24th GAWAD KALASAG Seal Awards. This prestigious recognition honors the dedication, resilience, and hard work of Ocampo’s local DRRM councils and offices in building safer communities through exceptional disaster risk reduction and management efforts. Financial commitment is evident, with funds allocated to constructing evacuation centers, relief operations, and DRRM training, although improvements are needed in resource allocation to expand emergency supplies and equipment, such as rescue boats. Residents, for the most part, cooperate during disasters, showing awareness and willingness to follow evacuation orders, although challenges remain with reluctant individuals. Recovery efforts, including road clearing, power restoration, distribution of relief goods, and provision of free medical services, demonstrate the municipality’s dedication to restoring normalcy post-typhoon. However, occasional gaps in equitable relief distribution indicate areas for enhancement. While Ocampo has made significant strides in disaster preparedness, integrating emerging technologies like AI for forecasting, improving resource accessibility, and enhancing transparency and inclusivity in planning and relief operations would further strengthen its capabilities. The overall readiness of the municipality is notable, with a clear commitment to safeguarding its residents and addressing the challenges posed by recurring typhoons. We rate Ocampo’s overall disaster preparedness as 8.
